Every other page in the privacy section makes a claim: that a call ran inside a sealed, hardware-isolated environment the host could not read or tamper with. Attestation is what turns that claim from a promise into something you can check. This section is the security-sensitive core of the Nito docs, so it is written to be read slowly and audited line by line. If you are a security reviewer, this is your map.

An **attestation** is a signed statement, produced by the hardware that ran your call, describing what was running inside the sealed environment. When you select Confidential or Encrypted (the `:confidential` or `:encrypted` suffix on a model ID), the provider can produce one of these statements, and Nito exposes it to you through a single endpoint: `GET /api/v1/attestation`. You hand the gateway a model and a fresh random nonce; it returns the raw evidence plus a structured summary, and it tells you whether the evidence passed its baseline checks.

## The one boundary to understand first

The Nito gateway is a **baseline verifier**, not a full hardware-quote verifier. This distinction is load-bearing, and it is repeated on every page in this section because misreading it is the single most dangerous mistake a reader could make.

The gateway checks that the evidence is present and well-formed, that the proof format and signing material are there, that the nonce you supplied is echoed back, and that the report-data binding holds. What the gateway does **not** do is verify the underlying hardware quote. It does not validate Intel TDX quotes, NVIDIA GPU evidence, certificate chains, certificate revocation lists (CRLs), or Trusted Computing Base (TCB) policies. That deeper verification is delegated to you, the client, through the verify-yourself path.

So the right words for a Nito attestation result are **baseline-verified**, **attested**, and **verifiable**. We never call it "trustless" or "fully verified," because the gateway alone does not establish full hardware trust. It gives you a fast baseline answer and hands you everything you need to do the rest yourself.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Generate a fresh nonce — you">
    Create a fresh random nonce for this request. Anti-replay is caller-driven, so a new nonce each time is what binds the attestation to this call.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Call the attestation endpoint — you">
    Send `GET /api/v1/attestation?model=…&nonce=…`, passing at minimum the model and your nonce.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Resolve the model — gateway">
    The gateway resolves the public model ID to an attestation-capable physical backend.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Call the provider attestation client — gateway">
    The gateway asks the model's TEE provider — NEAR or Phala — for its attestation evidence.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Run the baseline checks — gateway">
    The gateway verifies the evidence is present and parseable, the proof format and signing material are present, your nonce is echoed, and `report_data = signing_address + nonce`. It does **not** verify the underlying hardware quote.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Return evidence + summary — gateway">
    The gateway returns the raw `evidence`, a structured summary, and the `verified` flag.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Verify the hardware quote — you">
    On the raw evidence, you perform the full hardware verification yourself: Intel TDX, NVIDIA GPU evidence, cert chains, CRLs, and TCB.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Note>
  **You** own steps 1–2 and 7; the **gateway** owns 3–6. The gateway's baseline confirms only that your nonce is echoed and that `report_data = signing_address + nonce` — it does **not** verify Intel TDX, NVIDIA GPU evidence, cert chains, CRLs, or TCB. You do that in step 7, on the raw evidence it returns, which is why a Nito result is **baseline-verified**, never fully verified.
</Note>

## What anti-replay actually rests on

Attestation freshness on Nito is **caller-driven**. You supply a fresh random nonce on each request, and the gateway requires that nonce to appear in the provider's evidence and in the report-data binding. There is no server-side nonce store. If you omit the nonce, an empty-binding report is permitted. That means replay protection is something you create by always sending a fresh nonce; it is not an unconditional guarantee the gateway makes on your behalf. The report-data binding page covers this in full, and the verify-yourself recipe makes generating a fresh nonce step one.
