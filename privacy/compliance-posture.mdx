This page is deliberately conservative. Compliance claims, certifications, residency commitments, and regulatory alignment are statements with legal weight, and none of them are established in the codebase that these docs are built from. So this page does not assert any of them. It exists to draw a clean line between what is observably true in the system today and what is roadmap or aspiration, and to make clear that nothing in this section is published as a compliance claim until Legal and Security have reviewed and signed off.

<Warning>
Nothing on this page claims a certification or a regulatory status. Frameworks such as HIPAA, SOC 2, and EU data residency are **not asserted anywhere in code**, and they are not asserted here. Any final compliance language must be authored and approved by Legal and Security before publication.
</Warning>

## What is true today

These are the things the system actually does, which a compliance reviewer can verify and which any future compliance narrative would build on:

- **Metadata-only default retention.** Prompts, completions, reasoning, tool calls, and uploaded file contents are request-local and not persisted by the gateway. See the [Privacy overview](/privacy/overview).
- **Provider-side no-retention enforcement.** Per-request settings constrain each provider hop. See [Provider-side enforcement](/privacy/provider-side-enforcement).
- **Hashed credential storage.** Tokens are stored only as HMAC-SHA256 hashes, never in raw form.
- **Attested execution on the top tiers.** `tee` and `e2e-tee` modes run in sealed environments that produce verifiable attestations.
- **Standard web-security controls.** Security headers, CSRF protection, and attachment-URL restrictions are in place.

These are engineering facts, not compliance assertions. They describe behavior, not certification.

## How to read the gap between the two lists

The "what is true today" list is engineering behavior you can check. The "what is not asserted" list is everything that requires a legal or audit process to back it, which has not happened in a way these docs can cite. The gap between them is intentional and visible on purpose: it is more useful to a serious reviewer to see exactly where the verified facts end than to be handed a confident claim with nothing behind it.

If you are evaluating Nito under a specific framework, treat this page as a starting inventory of verifiable behavior, and route the formal questions to Legal and Security, who own every TODO above.
